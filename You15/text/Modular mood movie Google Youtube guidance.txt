Modular mood movie Google Youtube guidance

Your approach of using a programmatically designed, hours-long day/night cycle completely changes the game. It completely avoids the "low-effort, repetitive loop" trap because your visual content is continuously evolving across the entire duration.
YouTube’s automated system looks for short, lazy loops (like a 5-second GIF repeated for 6 hours). A continuous, progressive transition over multiple hours is viewed as high-production-value art.
Here is how to optimize this programmatic approach for YouTube's systems and ensure technical success:

1. Highlight the Evolving Nature in Metadata
Because YouTube's manual reviewers or AI content-rating systems might only click on random timestamps to check your video, you need to make it clear that the video is dynamically changing.
	•	Use Automated Timestamps: In your video description, add "Chapters" that correspond to the time of day in your video (e.g., 0:00 - Introduction, 0:10 - Morning Light, 1:30 - Midday Haze, 3:40 - Golden Hour, 5:00 - Twilight to Night).
	•	Describe the Tech: Explicitly mention in your description and on your channel "About" page that the visuals feature a custom, programmatically generated day/night cycle. YouTube rewards technical creativity and transparency about your creative process.

2. Solving the 10-Minute Intro Hurdle
Starting with a 10-minute linear introduction before the cycle begins is an excellent hook for viewer retention, but it introduces a rendering challenge.
	•	Code to Video: Since you mentioned the loops are "programmatically designed," you cannot stream raw code directly to a standard YouTube video upload. You will need to record or render the output of your program.
	•	Recording the Output: You can run your program locally on a powerful computer and use software like OBS Studio to record the screen at a high bitrate (at least 1080p or 4K at 30/60fps) for the full 6 hours. You can stitch the 10-minute intro to the front of this recording in a video editor.

3. The Audio-Visual Synchronization Challenge
Matching the ambient audio to a dynamic, evolving visual cycle requires careful planning, especially if the audio changes mood to match the time of day.
	•	Soundscape Transitions: Ensure your audio tracks are arranged to match the lighting. For example, your code should trigger crickets, owls, or deeper, moodier lo-fi chords when the visual cycle hits the "night" phase, and birds or brighter synth pads during the "morning" phase.
	•	Render as One File: To avoid any lag or desynchronization between your music and your programmatic visuals, record the audio and video together during your 6-hour capture session, rather than trying to line them up later in an editing software.

4. Handling YouTube's Compression
YouTube heavily compresses long videos, which can ruin smooth color gradients—like a slow sunset or a dark night sky—causing ugly pixel blocks (color banding).
	•	Introduce Grain/Texture: To stop YouTube's compression from ruining your smooth day/night transitions, layer a very subtle, high-quality "noise" or "film grain" overlay across the video. This forces YouTube’s video encoder to allocate more data to the gradients, keeping the transitions smooth and dreamlike.
