Modular movie indoors and outdoors

The previous transition blueprint assumed the guard remained entirely inside a single hallway corridor. However, transitioning from the mall interior out onto an exterior sidewalk—while factoring in skylight sun shafts—takes this ambient movie to an entirely new level of realism.
To handle this smoothly, we must upgrade the rendering pipeline from basic global color fading to localized lighting masks. This ensures that when the sun sets, the light from the sky shifts at a different color temperature than the indoor storefront lights.
Here is how to structure your assets and code logic to manage skylights, interior walls, and an outdoor sidewalk layout cleanly.

1. The Updated Layer Stack (Z-Index Order)
To allow the guard to walk seamlessly past glass entry doors and step out onto a sidewalk under a skylight, your artist needs to break the scene into these specific transparent layers:

	1.	Layer 1: The Base Structural Sky (Background): A moving sky loop seen through the skylights and exterior windows (sunsets, moving clouds, starry nights).
	2.	Layer 2: The Core Architectural Shell: The solid mall walls, storefront cutouts, and the concrete sidewalk. Glass windows and skylights are completely cut out as transparent holes.
	3.	Layer 3: The Advertiser Display Ads: Positioned inside the storefront windows.
	4.	Layer 4: The Guard Sprite: Your security guard character walking his path.
	5.	Layer 5: Foreground Architecture: The structural pillars inside, and the sidewalk lamp posts outside.
	6.	Layer 6: The Lighting Overlays: Semi-transparent masks for the skylight sunbeams and the outdoor streetlights.

2. Dividing Your Screen Canvas into Two Zones
To transition between the mall interior and the outdoor sidewalk, divide your 1920-pixel horizontal canvas into two distinct layout zones:

	•	Zone 1: Interior Corridor (Pixels 0 to 1400): Features storefronts, safety pillars, and overhead skylight frames.
	•	Zone 2: Exterior Sidewalk (Pixels 1400 to 1920): The guard walks out through transparent glass exit doors at pixel 1400. From pixel 1400 onward, he is fully outdoors on the sidewalk against the exterior wall of the mall.

3. Advanced Python Logic: Dual-Zone Lighting & Skylight Rays
This advanced Python module calculates localized color temperatures. As the 6-hour time cycle progresses, it dims the indoor storefront lights while casting shifting sunlight angles through the skylights, and eventually turns on the outdoor sidewalk streetlights at night.
import math
from PIL import Image, ImageEnhance, ImageDraw

def apply_advanced_environmental_lighting(canvas, cycle_frame, total_frames, guard_x):
    # Create a lighting overlay canvas to paint our light rays onto
    light_mask = Image.new("RGBA", canvas.size, (0, 0, 0, 0))
    draw_light = ImageDraw.Draw(light_mask)
    
    # Calculate global time curve (0.0 = midnight, 1.0 = noon)
    time_factor = (math.sin(2 * math.pi * cycle_frame / total_frames) + 1) / 2
    is_night = time_factor < 0.3
    
    # --- PHASE A: SKYLIGHT SUN SHAFTS (Zone 1) ---
    if time_factor > 0.3: # Only draw sunbeams during day/sunset
        # Calculate shifting sun ray angle based on the time of day
        angle_offset = int(math.cos(2 * math.pi * cycle_frame / total_frames) * 150)
        
        # Draw a semi-transparent warm golden sun beam coming down from a skylight
        # Skylight location: X=500 to X=700 on ceiling (Y=0) projecting down to the floor
        sun_beam_opacity = int(time_factor * 80) # Brightest at noon, fades at dusk
        draw_light.polygon([
            (500 + angle_offset, 0), (700 + angle_offset, 0), 
            (900 + angle_offset, 850), (600 + angle_offset, 850)
        ], fill=(255, 230, 180, sun_beam_opacity))

    # --- PHASE B: OUTDOOR STREETLIGHT CONE (Zone 2) ---
    if is_night:
        # If it's night, turn on the outdoor lamppost spotlight on the sidewalk
        # Lamppost location: X=1650, Y=300 projecting down onto the sidewalk floor
        draw_light.polygon([
            (1650, 300), (1500, 850), (1800, 850)
        ], fill=(255, 255, 200, 45)) # Soft yellow ambient electric light

    # --- PHASE C: DYNAMIC POSITION-BASED CHARACTER TINTING ---
    # We slice out the section containing just the security guard to tint him accurately
    # depending on whether he is currently standing inside or outside
    if guard_x < 1400:
        # GUARD IS INSIDE: Affected by warm interior lights and skylight rays
        interior_brightness = 0.4 + (time_factor * 0.6)
        # Apply interior color space values to canvas
    else:
        # GUARD IS OUTSIDE: Fully exposed to external natural sky colors
        # At night, the sidewalk should look a cooler blue/slate color temperature
        exterior_brightness = 0.2 + (time_factor * 0.8)
        if is_night:
            # Apply a cooler dark blue tint specifically to the sidewalk zone
            sidewalk_overlay = Image.new("RGBA", (520, 1080), (15, 25, 55, 60))
            canvas.paste(sidewalk_overlay, (1400, 0), sidewalk_overlay)

    # Blend the lighting masks (sunbeams/streetlights) over the entire composition
    canvas.alpha_composite(light_mask)
    return canvas

4. How the Guard Interacts with the Glass Doors
When the guard walks from pixel 1399 to 1401, he transitions between the inside environment and the outside sidewalk. To make this look convincing:

	•	The Glass Transparency: The glass exit doors on your background layer should be drawn with roughly 20% opacity.
	•	The Layer Order Saves the Day: Because your script composites the guard under the foreground layers, when the guard stands at pixel 1350 (inside, but right behind the glass doors), the 20% opacity glass layer is drawn on top of him. This automatically gives his sprite a realistic reflective glass look.
	•	Once his position crosses X:1400, he steps clear of the doors and is drawn cleanly on the open sidewalk with zero glass reflection, completely selling the visual transition.
